Copyright Act, 1957 · §13

What copyright actually protects

Original literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works, plus cinematograph films and sound recordings — this includes books, articles, blog posts, photographs, paintings, software code, music, and video. It protects the specific expression of an idea, not the underlying idea, fact, or method itself.

§13(1)

Copyright arises automatically — registration is optional

Unlike a trademark or patent, copyright exists the moment an original work is created and fixed in some tangible form (written down, saved, recorded) — no registration is required for the right to exist. Registration with the Copyright Office (via copyright.gov.in, Form XIV) is voluntary, but strongly advisable anyway: it creates an official, dated public record that's far easier to rely on as evidence of ownership and creation date if a dispute ever reaches court.

§22 / §25 / §26

How long copyright lasts

For literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works: the author's lifetime + 60 years thereafter. For cinematograph films, sound recordings, photographs, and works published anonymously or by a corporate/government body: 60 years from the year of publication.

§52

Fair dealing — what's allowed without permission

Private/personal use including research, criticism or review, reporting current events, use in judicial proceedings, and certain educational/library uses are all exceptions to infringement — but "fair dealing" in India is narrower and more fact-specific than US "fair use"; how much you use and why matters.

§51 & §63

If someone infringes your copyright

You can pursue civil remedies (injunction, damages, account of profits) before a District Court, and criminal remedies (imprisonment up to 3 years, fine) since infringement is a cognizable, non-bailable offence under §63. For online infringement, platforms (YouTube, Instagram, etc.) also have their own takedown-request processes you can use in parallel — often faster than litigation for a first response.

Patents Act, 1970 · §2(1)(j)

What makes an invention patentable

Three conditions, all required: it must be novel (not disclosed anywhere in the world before your filing date — including your own public demo or paper), involve an inventive step (not obvious to someone skilled in the field), and have industrial applicability (it can actually be made or used in an industry).

§7 & §9

Provisional vs. complete specification

Most applicants file a provisional specification first — it locks in a priority date cheaply while the invention is still being finalised — then must file the complete specification within 12 months, or the application is treated as abandoned.

§11A & §11B

Timeline: publication, examination & grant

Applications are published 18 months after filing (or earlier, on request). Examination only happens after you separately file a Request for Examination (RFE) — within 48 months of the priority date. Recent Patent Office reforms have meaningfully cut the time to first examination report and grant compared to a few years ago, but a contested application can still take several years to grant.

Fee schedule

Who pays less

Natural person / startup / small entity applicants pay substantially lower official fees than large entities — roughly an 80% reduction for individuals and up to 60% for startups/small entities, at every stage (filing, RFE, and beyond). Confirm current fee slabs on the IP India portal before filing, as they're revised periodically.

Step 1

Register everything that matters early

Trademark (word mark + logo separately, in every class you actually operate or plan to expand into), the matching domain name(s), and social media handles — squatting on any of these by a third party is far more expensive to undo than to prevent. Copyright registers itself automatically on creation, but register formally for anything commercially important (packaging design, brand copy, software).

Trade Marks Act §21

Watch for and oppose conflicting applications

Once your mark is registered, monitor the weekly Trade Marks Journal for identical/similar marks being filed by others in your class — you have 4 months from the date of journal publication to file a Notice of Opposition. Miss the window and a conflicting mark can proceed to registration.

§27 & §29

Passing off vs. infringement

If your mark is registered, you sue for infringement (easier to prove — just show a deceptively similar mark on similar goods). If it's unregistered, you can still sue for "passing off" at common law, but you additionally have to prove you've built real goodwill/reputation in the mark — harder and slower. This is the single biggest practical reason to register early rather than rely on first use alone.

Renewal

Don't let registration lapse

A registered trademark must be renewed every 10 years (Form TM-R), indefinitely — miss the renewal (plus a limited restoration grace period) and the mark can be removed from the register entirely, opening the door for someone else to claim it.

.in / INDRP

Domain name disputes

If someone registers a confusingly similar .in domain to squat on or trade off your brand, the INDRP (.IN Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy) offers a faster, cheaper route than a civil suit to get it transferred — broadly similar in spirit to ICANN's UDRP for generic top-level domains.

Enforcement

When someone infringes anyway

Start with a cease-and-desist legal notice (fast, cheap, often enough on its own) before escalating to a civil suit for injunction and damages before the relevant District/High Court, or a criminal complaint under the Trade Marks Act for counterfeiting. For online marketplaces, most also have their own IP-complaint takedown processes worth using in parallel.
